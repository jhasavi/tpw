## CASE STUDY PROFILES

### Sarah (32, Single, Earning $65k/year)
**Background:**
- Graphic designer at mid-size marketing firm
- Single, no dependents
- Lives in mid-size city (moderate cost of living)
- Student loans from design degree

**Financial Situation:**
- Gross income: $65,000/year
- Net (after taxes, benefits): ~$4,100/month
- Fixed expenses: $2,500/month
  - Rent: $1,200
  - Student loans: $300
  - Car payment: $350
  - Insurance: $200
  - Utilities: $150
  - Phone/Internet: $100
  - Subscriptions: $200
- Variable expenses: ~$1,000/month
  - Groceries: $300
  - Gas: $150
  - Dining out: $200
  - Entertainment: $150
  - Clothing: $100
  - Misc: $100
- Emergency fund: $3,000 (goal: $10k)
- Debt: $18,000 student loans at 5%

**Financial Goals:**
1. Build 6-month emergency fund ($15k)
2. Pay off student loans in 5 years
3. Save for home down payment ($40k in 7 years)
4. Start retirement investing (401k + Roth IRA)

**Challenges:**
- Feels overwhelmed by multiple goals
- Doesn't know how to prioritize
- Thinks investing is too complicated
- Worried she's "behind" peers

**Lesson Applications:**
- Budgeting: How to allocate remaining $600/month
- Debt payoff: 5-year plan to eliminate $18k
- Emergency fund: 3-year timeline at $300/month
- Investing: Start small with Roth IRA

**Outcomes (if implements lessons):**
- Year 1: Emergency fund $5,700 + student loans down to $14,700
- Year 3: Emergency fund complete + student loans $7,500
- Year 5: Student loans eliminated + $10k Roth IRA started
- Year 7: $40k down payment saved

## HOW TO USE THESE CASE STUDIES

### In Lesson Content
```
## Real Example: Sarah's Budget

Sarah is a 32-year-old graphic designer earning $65,000/year.
Here's her actual situation:

**Income:**
- Gross: $65,000/year ($5,417/month)
- After taxes: ~$4,100/month

**Fixed Expenses:** $2,500/month
- Rent: $1,200
- Car: $350
- Student loans: $300
- Insurance: $200
- Utilities: $150
- Other: $100 + $100 subscriptions

**Sarah's Challenge:** 
She has $4,100 - $2,500 = $1,600 left, but it goes to food, gas, 
and fun. Nothing left for emergency fund, investing, or debt payoff. 
Where does she find money?

**Sarah's Solution (using lesson strategies):**
1. Cut subscriptions ($100) ‚Üí "I don't use 3 of them"
2. Reduce dining out ($100) ‚Üí Pack lunch 2x/week
3. Find roommate? ($300) ‚Üí Too much, but exploring...
4. Ask for raise ($3k/year) ‚Üí Ask for promotion in 6 months

Now Sarah has: Original $1,600 + $200 cuts = $1,800
- Emergency fund: $300/month (3 years to goal)
- Debt payoff: $300/month
- Investing: $100/month

YOUR SITUATION: What does your budget look like?
```

### Quiz Questions
```
Question: Sarah's fixed expenses are $2,500/month and she nets $4,100.
How much is available for variable expenses and savings?
A) $1,600
```

## SCENARIO-BASED QUESTIONS

### Scenario: The Unexpected Emergency
**Context:** Maria (divorced, 2 kids, $3,600/month net)

"It's Tuesday. Your car breaks down. Repair: $1,200. You have $500 in emergency fund.
What do you do?"

A) Pay the full $1,200 on credit card (0% promo)
B) Delay repair, save $1,200 first
C) Ask family for loan
D) Use credit card for full amount

**Discussion:**
- What's the real cost of each option?
- What if you have no credit card?
- How would having a full emergency fund ($12k) change this?

### Scenario: The Investing Question
**Context:** Jessica (married, $10k/month net, 30 years to retirement)

"You have $200/month extra. Your coworker says:
'Investing is too risky, keep cash in savings account.'
Your partner says: 'Stocks are the only way to get rich.'

What's the right answer?"

A) Listen to coworker (safety first)
B) Listen to partner (go big)
C) Split ($100 stocks, $100 savings)
D) Wait until you "learn more"

**Discussion:**
- Timeline matters (30 years = can take risk)
- $200/month at 7% vs 4% = $64k difference
- Fear prevents wealth building
